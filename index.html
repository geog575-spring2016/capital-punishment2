The history of Americaâ€™s laws on capital punishment has been inconsistent and indefinite. In 1972, capital punishment was suspended in the judicial Furman v. Georgia ruling. However, it resumed in 1976 with a case of first degree murder in North Carolina (Woodson vs. Roberts) which brought to the United States Supreme Court. The United, States suspended the death penalty again in September 2007 but it resumed again in April 2008. America is one of the only Western countries that still uses capital punishment in our justice system. In 2015, the United States had the fifth highest death penalty rate with 28 people being killed, following China, Iran, Pakistan and Saudi Arabia.

Many believe that this is an archaic practice and America has put laws in place to try and modernize this outdated practice by making it unconstitutional to execute anyone under the age or 16 and of insane persons. However, there are some obsolete modes of executions such as; burning, firing squad, hanging, and gas, now electrocution and injection are most common. The scariest part of the death penalty is the amount of wrongful convictions. There has been a decrease in the number of people convicted for the death penalty and the number of executions that have been carried out, which shows us the direction that America is moving toward.

About Us

Should the Death Penalty be Put to Death? is a collaboration between Natalee Desotell, Kai Brito, and Gaby Barrett. We created this interactive for Robert Roth and Robin Tolochko's Spring 2016 Interactive Cartography & Geovisualization course at the University of Wisconsin-Madison.

A big thanks goes out to Robert Roth, Robin Tolochko for their help. The design of this project was inspired by Your Body, (Not) Your Choice, created by Robin Tolochko, Katie Kowalsky, and Dylan Moriarty.

We created this project with the D3 library, jQuery, jQuery Timer and Bootstrap.

If you find any errors in our data or just have general feedback, please get in touch.
